What Doesn't Work

Debunking fitness myths and fad diets with science

The Problem with Fad Diets

The fitness industry thrives on selling quick fixes and magic solutions. But the research is clear: these approaches don't optimize the metrics that actually matter for long-term health.

Common Myths Debunked

Myth: Avoiding specific foods (gluten, dairy, seed oils) will transform your health

Reality: Unless you have a specific medical condition (celiac disease, lactose intolerance), eliminating food groups won't meaningfully impact your strength, VO2 max, LDL, blood pressure, or body fat percentage.

What actually matters: Total caloric intake, adequate protein, whole food quality, and overall dietary patterns.

Myth: Keto/Paleo/Carnivore diets are superior for fat loss

Reality: When protein and calories are controlled, no diet shows superior fat loss outcomes. The "best" diet is the one you can sustain while meeting your protein needs and maintaining a caloric deficit.

What actually matters: Caloric deficit, high protein intake, and adherence.

Content coming soon - Meta-analyses comparing diet types with controlled protein and calories

Myth: Detoxes and cleanses improve metabolic health

Reality: Your liver and kidneys handle detoxification. Juice cleanses and detox teas don't improve LDL, blood pressure, or body composition. They often result in muscle loss and metabolic slowdown.

What actually matters: Consistent healthy eating, adequate hydration, and supporting organ function through overall lifestyle.

Content coming soon - Research on metabolic effects of cleanses

Myth: Cardio is all you need for fitness

Reality: While cardio improves VO2 max, it doesn't build significant strength or muscle mass. You need resistance training to optimize strength and body composition.

What actually matters: Both cardiovascular exercise and resistance training are essential for comprehensive health.

Content coming soon - Studies on cardio-only vs. combined training

Myth: Supplements can replace proper training and nutrition

Reality: Most supplements have minimal impact compared to training, diet, and sleep. Fat burners, BCAAs, and most pre-workouts won't move the needle on the metrics that matter.

What actually matters: Progressive resistance training, adequate protein, caloric balance, and recovery.

Content coming soon - Research on supplement efficacy vs. lifestyle factors

Myth: You can spot reduce fat

Reality: Doing endless crunches won't reduce belly fat. Fat loss happens systemically based on genetics and overall caloric deficit.

What actually matters: Total body fat reduction through caloric deficit and resistance training to maintain muscle.

Content coming soon - Studies on spot reduction vs. whole-body fat loss

Myth: Eating frequently "boosts metabolism"

Reality: Meal frequency doesn't significantly impact metabolic rate or fat loss when total calories and protein are equal. Eat on whatever schedule works for you.

What actually matters: Total daily calories and protein intake, not timing.

Focus on What Works

Instead of chasing trends, focus on the fundamentals that actually optimize your health metrics:

  • Progressive resistance training 2-4x per week for strength
  • Cardiovascular exercise for VO2 max improvement
  • Adequate protein intake (1.6-2.2g/kg bodyweight)
  • Caloric balance appropriate for your goals
  • Consistent sleep (7-9 hours nightly)
  • Stress management for blood pressure and overall health
  • Regular monitoring of your key metrics

These aren't sexy. They don't sell books or supplements. But they're what the science consistently shows actually works.
