Getting Started

Our evidence-based approach to fitness and health

Our Philosophy

The fitness industry is saturated with misinformation, fad diets, and quick-fix solutions. Every week there's a new superfood, miracle supplement, or diet trend promising to revolutionize your health.

This site exists to cut through the noise and focus on what the scientific evidence actually shows matters for long-term health and longevity.

We believe in:

  • Evidence over anecdote: Peer-reviewed research trumps testimonials
  • Fundamentals over fads: The basics work better than tricks and hacks
  • Metrics over aesthetics: Health markers predict longevity better than appearance
  • Honesty over hype: No magic pills, no shortcuts, no nonsense
  • Accessibility over complexity: Simple, actionable information anyone can use

Step 1: Identify the Metrics That Matter

So you want to live longer, feel good, and look great. There are a million programs out there that promise this outcome. But, if you think about it, those are very subjective and immeasurable goals. How do you really know if what you are doing is really moving the needle on those goals? That latest miracle supplement that you heard about -- how do you really know it's going to help you live longer? That high protein diet you started -- is it really adding muscle? Before you start a new program, it's important to identify goals that can be measured objectively.

This site outlines five metrics that matter. These are science-backed metrics that proportionally (that means, the better you get at them the better they work) improve health outcomes. Each section on the site will have easy to understand data graphs as well as links to the science-backed research so you can confirm for yourself that the information is accurate.

The Five Metrics

  • Strength -- Muscular strength is one of the strongest predictors of all-cause mortality. It maintains independence as you age, protects against metabolic disease, and preserves bone density. The stronger you are, the longer and better you live.
  • VO2 Max -- Your cardiovascular fitness level is the gold standard for predicting longevity. Research shows that moving from the bottom 25% to even average fitness dramatically reduces your mortality risk. It's the single most powerful predictor of how long you'll live.
  • Body Fat Percentage -- Body composition matters far more than what the scale says. Excess body fat, especially visceral fat around your organs, drives chronic inflammation and metabolic dysfunction. Maintaining a healthy body fat percentage reduces your risk for nearly every chronic disease.
  • LDL Cholesterol -- LDL cholesterol is the primary driver of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, the number one killer worldwide. Managing your LDL through diet, exercise, and when necessary medication, is one of the most impactful things you can do for long-term health.
  • Blood Pressure -- Hypertension is a silent killer that damages your blood vessels, heart, kidneys, and brain over time. Keeping your blood pressure in a healthy range significantly reduces your risk of heart attack, stroke, and kidney disease.

What About the Metrics That Are Missing?

There is a section on the site for "What Doesn't Work". You may be wondering about that latest fad -- the miracle supplement, the trendy diet, the biohacking gadget. We'll cover that stuff there. If something has solid evidence behind it, it would already be reflected in these five metrics. If it doesn't move the needle on any of them, it's probably not worth your time or money.

Step 2: Follow a Simple, Evidence-Based Plan

Once we understand the metrics we are aiming for, next we need a plan to achieve them. You've no doubt already tried many exercise routines and diets in the past -- what makes this different?

The primary focus is on Minimum Effective Dose -- meaning, how can we achieve the outcomes that we want with the minimum amount of effort. This might rub some people the wrong way -- if I try harder, can't I get there faster? In this case -- no. The human body can only add so much muscle per week and per year -- and you don't add that muscle at the gym -- you add it after the gym when you are recovering. Training more doesn't make you gain faster, it just makes you more fatigued, more prone to injury, and more likely to give up.

What about diet? You've no doubt tried excluding all the bad foods or intermittent fasting or keto or whatever the latest trend is. But you also know that 95% of diets fail. The truth is that all of the fad diets work -- follow them and you will lose weight. The problem is that they are not sustainable. The plan outlined on this site is one that lets you enjoy all the foods you like, even the bad ones, and that you can follow for life.

Each of the five metric pages on this site includes a detailed, science-backed plan. These plans are designed to be simple, sustainable, and effective. No complicated periodization schemes, no expensive equipment, no foods you need to eliminate forever. Just the fundamentals, done consistently.

Step 3: Track Your Progress

So you now understand that you need measurable goals, that you need a diet and exercise routine that is easy to adhere to in order to achieve these goals over the long term. Now you need to track your progress.

The truth is that progress takes time. You can achieve your goals of looking and feeling great -- but it won't happen overnight. Tracking your progress will show you how small gains week by week add up to the big goals. When you see your strength numbers slowly climbing, your VO2 max improving, your body fat percentage dropping -- even by small amounts -- it reinforces that the process is working. It keeps you motivated when you can't yet see the changes in the mirror.

Tracking also keeps you honest. It's easy to convince yourself that things are going well when you're not measuring. But data doesn't lie. If your numbers are stalling, it tells you something needs to change. If they're improving, it tells you to keep doing what you're doing.

This site provides practical tracking tools and templates so you can monitor each of the five metrics over time. The system is designed to be simple -- just a few minutes per week -- so that tracking itself doesn't become another burden that makes you want to quit.
